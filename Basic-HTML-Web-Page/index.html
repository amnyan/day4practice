Is Programming Science Just a Myth?

In a world dominated by technological advances, one might assume that programming, as a discipline, holds the esteemed status of a rigorous science. However, upon closer examination, it's worth questioning whether programming science is truly as scientific as it claims to be.
1. Lack of Universal Theories:
Unlike traditional sciences that are grounded in universal theories and principles—such as gravity in physics or evolution in biology—programming lacks universally accepted theories. The field is filled with numerous programming paradigms, languages, and methodologies, each with its own set of "rules" and best practices. This diversity often leads to competing standards rather than a unified body of knowledge.
2. Subjectivity and Personal Preferences:
Programming often relies on subjective decisions and personal preferences. What one developer considers a best practice might be deemed inefficient by another. The variance in coding styles and approaches can be seen as a departure from the objectivity typically associated with scientific disciplines.
3. Rapid Evolution and Obsolescence:
The field of programming evolves at a breakneck pace, with languages and frameworks quickly becoming obsolete. This rapid change makes it difficult to establish lasting scientific principles. In contrast, scientific fields like chemistry or astronomy rely on stable, long-standing principles that withstand the test of time.
The Myth of Perfect Solutions:
In science, theories are tested and refined through rigorous experimentation. However, in programming, solutions are often seen as imperfect and subject to continual improvement. The idea that there is a "perfect" code or algorithm is more of a myth than a reality, highlighting the inherent limitations and uncertainties in programming.
5. Absence of Empirical Validation:

While programming involves empirical practices, the validation process is not always as methodical as in traditional sciences. The success of a program often relies on trial and error, debugging, and user feedback rather than controlled experiments and repeatable results.
In conclusion, while programming is undoubtedly a critical and complex field, labeling it as a "science" might be an overstatement. The lack of universal theories, subjective practices, rapid evolution, and the myth of perfect solutions suggest that programming operates more like an art or craft than a strict scientific discipline. It's essential to recognize these distinctions to better understand the nature of programming and its place in our technological landscape.
